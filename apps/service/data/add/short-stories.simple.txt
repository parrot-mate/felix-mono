# THE MURDERS IN THE RUE MORGUE - Edgar Allan Poe
The thinking skills known as 'analytical' are hard to explain. We only see their effects. We understand that these skills bring great joy to those who have them in large amounts. Like a strong person enjoys showing their strength, someone who analyzes well enjoys solving problems. They find joy even in small tasks that use their skills. They like puzzles and tricky questions, and they solve them in ways that seem almost magical to others. Their methods are so good, their answers seem like guesses.
Studying math, especially its highest part often called analysis, may strengthen our ability to make decisions. However, calculating numbers is not the same as analyzing situations. For example, a chess player can make moves without deeply analyzing the game. People often misunderstand how chess affects thinking. I'm not writing a detailed study, but just sharing some thoughts before telling a special story. I believe that playing the simple game of draughts (checkers) challenges our thinking more than the complicated game of chess. In chess, the pieces move in complex ways which can confuse complexity with depth. You need to pay close attention because a small mistake can cause you to lose. There are many possible moves in chess, increasing the chance of errors. Often, the player who focuses better wins, not necessarily the smarter one. In draughts, the moves are simpler and less varied, reducing the chance of mistakes. The players win by being sharper thinkers. To explain better, imagine a draughts game with only four kings left. No mistakes are expected here. Victory depends only on clever moves which require strong thinking. Without usual strategies, the analyst must fully understand the opponent's thinking and quickly find the best way to win, sometimes using very simple tactics.
Whist is a card game known for improving thinking skills. Smart people enjoy it even though they might find chess too simple. Whist requires strong analytical skills, which means it helps in understanding complex situations. Being good at whist shows you can be successful in other challenging activities where you need to think deeply. In whist, remembering the rules and past plays is important, but being a great player is about more than just following rules. It involves noticing small details and making smart guesses based on those details. For example, a skilled player watches the faces of other players and how they handle their cards to guess their strategies. They pay attention to everything, not just the game, to make the best decisions. This helps them predict the outcome of the game and play their cards with confidence.
Being analytical is different from just being clever. While someone who analyzes well must be clever, a clever person might not be good at analyzing. The ability to combine ideas, which shows cleverness, is thought to be a basic skill by some scientists, but this is probably wrong. This skill is often found in people who are otherwise not very smart, which many writers have noticed. The difference between being clever and being able to analyze is big, much like the difference between having simple creative ideas and having deep imagination, but they are similar in some ways. Usually, clever people are full of creative ideas, and truly imaginative people are always good at analyzing. The story I'm going to tell will help explain these ideas more. When I was in Paris in the spring and summer of a certain year, I met a man named Monsieur C. Auguste Dupin. He came from a very good family, but had become poor because of many bad situations. He stopped trying to improve his situation because he felt overwhelmed. He still had a little bit of money left from his family, and he lived on this money very carefully, just buying what he really needed. The only extra things he spent money on were books, which are easy to get in Paris.
We first met in a small library on Rue Montmartre because we were both looking for the same rare book, and this made us become friends. We met several times after that. I was very interested in his family stories which he shared openly, a common thing for French people when talking about themselves. I was also amazed by how much he had read and was inspired by his lively and creative imagination. While I was in Paris, I felt that having him as a friend was very valuable, and I told him so. Eventually, we decided to live together while I was in the city. Since I had more money than he did, I paid for us to live in a big, old house that matched our moody style, even though it was falling apart and located in a quiet, neglected part of Faubourg St. Germain. If people knew how we lived there, they might think we were crazy, but harmless. We didn't see anyone else while we were there. I kept our location a secret from people I knew before, and Dupin hadn't been in touch with anyone in Paris for many years. It was just the two of us.
My friend had a strange fascination with the night and loved it for its own sake. I always went along with his unusual ideas and enjoyed them completely. We pretended night was always with us. Every morning, we closed all the shutters in our old house and lit some scented candles that gave off a weak light. We spent our time dreaming, reading, writing, or talking until it was really dark. Then we would walk together in the streets, talking or exploring the city late into the night, looking for excitement that just watching quietly could give us. During these walks, I noticed that Dupin, my friend, was very good at analyzing things. He enjoyed using this skill and wasn't shy about saying how much he liked it. He often told me that he could read people easily, as if they were open books, and he proved it to me in surprising ways. When he was like this, he seemed distant and his eyes looked empty. His voice, usually deep, would get higher, but he spoke clearly and carefully. Watching him, I would think about the old idea that each person has two sides to their soul, and I imagined Dupin had two sides too—one creative and one analytical.

If the shutter was fully open, a thief could grab the grid, let go of the rod, secure his feet on the wall, and jump towards the shutter to close it.
